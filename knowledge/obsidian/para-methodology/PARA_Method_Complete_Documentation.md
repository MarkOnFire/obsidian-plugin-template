## Core Framework

The PARA Method organizes digital information into four categories:

### **1. Projects** - Short-term efforts with specific goals

**Definition**: Projects are tasks with a deadline and defined outcome.

**Examples**:
- Complete webpage design
- Buy a new computer
- Renovate the bathroom

**Characteristics**:
- Time-bound
- Specific deliverable
- Clear completion criteria

---

### **2. Areas** - Ongoing responsibilities requiring continuous attention

**Definition**: Areas are spheres of activity with standards to maintain over time, but no end date.

**Work Examples**:
- Marketing
- HR
- Product Management
- R&D

**Personal Examples**:
- Health
- Finances
- Kids
- Home
- Car

**Characteristics**:
- No defined endpoint
- Requires ongoing attention
- Measured by standards, not completion

---

### **3. Resources** - Topics of interest for learning and reference

**Definition**: Resources are topics or themes of ongoing interest that you want to learn about or reference later.

**Examples**:
- Graphic design
- Productivity
- Gardening
- Architecture
- Languages

**Characteristics**:
- Reference materials
- Not immediately actionable
- Organized by topic/theme
- Support future projects and areas

---

### **4. Archives** - Inactive items from previous categories

**Definition**: Archives contain inactive items from the other three categories, preserved for future reference.

**Characteristics**:
- No longer active
- Historical record
- Searchable when needed
- Out of the way

## Key Principles

### Actionability Over Subject Categories

**Core Philosophy**: Organize by what you need to DO, not what things ARE.

As Tiago Forte explains: "organize it according to the projects and goals you are committed to right now."

**Why This Matters**:
- Reduces decision fatigue
- Makes next actions obvious
- Eliminates endless hierarchical categorization
- Focuses on current priorities

**Traditional Approach (Subject-Based)**:
```
- Work
  - HR
  - Marketing
  - Product
- Personal
  - Health
  - Finance
  - Home
```

**PARA Approach (Action-Based)**:
```
- Projects (What I'm working on now)
- Areas (What I maintain)
- Resources (What I reference)
- Archives (What's complete)
```

---

### Breaking Down Areas Into Projects

Two critical benefits emerge from decomposing areas into specific projects:

#### **1. Clarity of Commitments** - Understanding actual workload

**The Problem**: Areas can hide actual workload.

**Example**:
- **Vague Area**: "Hiring"
- **Broken Down Into Projects**:
  - Fill senior engineer position
  - Recruit summer interns
  - Improve interview process

**Result**: Clear view of actual time commitments and priorities.

#### **2. Motivation Through Progress** - Regular psychological wins

**The Problem**: Areas never end, which can feel demotivating.

**The Solution**: Projects within areas create "a cadence of regular victories."

**Example**:
- **Endless Area**: Health
- **Completable Projects**:
  - Complete 30-day yoga challenge
  - Run 5K race
  - Meal prep for two weeks

**Result**: Sense of progress and accomplishment while maintaining ongoing responsibilities.

## Implementation Philosophy

### Minimal by Design

**Core Principle**: "Your system has to give you time, not take time."

**What PARA Avoids**:
- Complex hierarchies
- Endless categorization
- Procrastination disguised as organizing
- Over-engineering

**What PARA Focuses On**:
- Supporting next actionable steps
- Fast decisions (only 4 categories)
- Easy maintenance
- Practical utility

**Tiago Forte's Guidance**:
PARA avoids the procrastination disguised as organizing by focusing exclusively on what supports your next actionable steps—nothing more.

## Practical Implementation

### Decision Tree

When adding new information, ask:

1. **Is this related to a project I'm actively working on?**
   → YES: Add to **Projects**

2. **Is this related to an area I'm responsible for?**
   → YES: Add to **Areas**

3. **Is this something I want to learn about or reference later?**
   → YES: Add to **Resources**

4. **Is this from a completed project or inactive area?**
   → YES: Add to **Archives**

### Movement Between Categories

**Project → Archive**: When project completes
**Resource → Project**: When you start working on something
**Area → Project**: When you define specific deliverable with deadline
**Project → Area**: When ongoing maintenance work emerges from completed project

## Common Pitfalls

### Pitfall 1: Over-Categorizing Resources
**Problem**: Creating too many detailed subcategories in Resources
**Solution**: Keep broad categories, use search/tags to find specific items

### Pitfall 2: Never Archiving
**Problem**: Projects and Areas become cluttered with inactive items
**Solution**: Archive aggressively; you can always search if needed

### Pitfall 3: Treating Areas Like Projects
**Problem**: Trying to "complete" ongoing responsibilities
**Solution**: Break down specific outcomes into projects under that area

### Pitfall 4: Inbox Bloat
**Problem**: Never processing inbox, letting it become permanent storage
**Solution**: Regular review cycles (daily/weekly) to categorize new items

## Comparison with Other Systems

### vs. GTD (Getting Things Done)
- **GTD**: Focus on tasks and next actions
- **PARA**: Focus on organizing information *around* projects and goals
- **Compatibility**: PARA organizes *reference materials*, GTD organizes *actions*

### vs. Subject-Based Filing
- **Subject-Based**: "What category does this belong to?"
- **PARA**: "What am I doing with this?"
- **Key Difference**: Actionability vs. taxonomy

### vs. Zettelkasten
- **Zettelkasten**: Network of interconnected atomic notes
- **PARA**: Hierarchical organization by actionability
- **Compatibility**: Can use PARA as top-level structure with Zettelkasten within
